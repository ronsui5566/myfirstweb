Abstract

This study addresses the limitations of traditional STEM education by integrating socio-scientific issues within the STEM-5E instructional framework, using generative AI-driven scaffolding for self-regulated learning (GAISSR). We investigated how different types of GAISSR affect students' argumentation performance over time and how students' epistemic beliefs about generative AI (GenAI) relate to their improvement in argumentation skills. Ninety-four eighth-grade students participated, randomly assigned to metacognitive GAISSR, cognitive GAISSR, or a control group. Findings revealed a significant interaction between scaffolding type and task sequence; cognitive scaffolding initially improved performance but did not sustain gains, whereas metacognitive scaffolding supported continued improvement by the final task.

Scaffolding Effect

Metacognitive scaffolding promoted deeper, enduring argumentation skills through self-assessment and reflective prompts.

Epistemic Beliefs

Students who improved held stronger beliefs in certainty (pd=99.97%), source, and structure of GenAI content.

Learning Trajectory

Cognitive scaffolding showed early gains that plateaued; metacognitive scaffolding sustained improvement.

Implications for Practice

Metacognitive Scaffolding

Leverage GenAI to deliver reflective prompts following self-assessment for deeper cognitive engagement.

Calibrated Trust

Foster sophisticated understanding of GenAI capabilities and limitations for critical appraisal skills.
